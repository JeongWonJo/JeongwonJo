Project Overview

📌 Objectives

  • Exploring how community-based disaster relief was coordinated online and offline, engaging locals with different resources
  • Ideating design recommendations that can support sustainable , diverse, and extensive local community-based disaster relief

🔎  Methods

  • Interview Study

🙌  My Role

  • Principal investigator
    • Collaborated with two other researchers
    • Interviewed 17 participants
    • Thematically coded interview data
    • Ideated design recommendations
    • Wrote and published an academic paper in CSCW 2023

Coproduction by Elinor Ostrom

🧐 Why Coproduction?

Our data showed that locals played an active role in producing and reciprocally exchanging support, regardless of when they joined, reflecting coproduction

Unilateral disaster relief delivery was sufficient in previous disasters, where no or less affected citizens (providers) offered support to those affected (recipients). However, because everyone was affected by the immediate biological or broader social consequences of COVID-19, unilateral support was insufficient.

Expected benefits of coproduction include more responsive participants, reduced costs, and the increase in the quality, quantity, and diversity of service

A deeper understanding of how citizens coproduced disaster relief can lay the groundwork for more extensive, sustained, and diverse relief

🔎 Method

1. Collecting Local Community Disaster Relief

Data Collection: 10 research members individually added local community based-disaster relief cases from online articles in public press, social media, or email listservs to a shared spreadsheet.
‍
Weekly peer debriefing sessions: to share how each case was operated, what support it produced, and who benefited from it, and how similar or different each case was from others.

Grouping cases: referred to the indices of community resilience to disasters, consisted of Economic Resilience, Infrastructure Resilience, and Community Capital; continued to collect data for the groupings that had fewer cases, ending up with 158 cases.

2. Interviews

Selecting interview targets: innovative disaster relief cases, those with emergent structural arrangements in operation, resources utilized, or tasks and activities engaged in.

Semi-structured interviews: 10 semi-structured interview sessions with 14 organizers and 3 sessions with 3 volunteers.

‍Predefined questions for organizers:
• How was your disaster relief group initiated?
• How did your group support community members? What feedback did they provide?
• What challenges did your group face, and how were they overcome
• What are future aspirations or plans of your group?

‍Predefined questions for volunteers:
• How did you hear about the relief activities?
• Can you tell me about your motives and experience with participating in relief activities?

3. Thematic Analysis of Interview Data

We individually performed open coding, made an affinity diagram to group codes and create themes, and collectively generated a codebook.

Findings

1. Challenges in Identifying Those in Need

Challenge: Those in need without digital devices or the Internet were not aware of support initiatives; hard for civic initiatives to identify people who needed support

"A lot of people who were looking for this kind of service don’t necessarily have a smartphone or Internet access...and won’t be familiar with[our initiative]" (P3)

"a challenge [was] in trying to figure out where [we were] needed the most" (P12)

→ Solution: Collaboration with established mature local organizations

"We find it easier to work through institutions rather than ending it up to individual...we are working through the schools, ..., Parents for Homeschooling, ..., and the [city recreation]center, so we don’t have to make judgments about who deserves one, who doesn’t." (P5)

‍

Challenge: Digital illiteracy of those in need

"There were so many [elderly] who couldn’t use the app [for the grocery delivery volunteer service]." (P2)

→ Solution:

  1. A feature that allowed someone else to submit support requests on behalf of those who needed support
  2. Virtual digital training by sending a booklet and Zoom tutorials
  3. Simple interfaces with easy-to-understand instructions
"[made] the website purposely simple so that anyone who has access can figure it out and use it"(P3)

‍

Challenge: Hard to convince people who did not want to own a digital device that doing so would help them out

"I had to do so much pushing on the recipient-end...I almost had to beg[people] to say,
‘Yes, I would like a computer’" (P4)

‍

2. Challenges in Finding and Engaging Volunteers

Challenge: Face-to-face contact was reduced, impeding the progress of recruiting new volunteers

"[We couldn't] grab someone in the hallway...[and] accost anyone to try and get anyone to do what [we] needed"(P7)

→ Solution:

  1. Getting the word out about their initiatives online
"We had a press release out, we were on a Podcast, ..., [we] promoted in...college department social media and websites, ..., we are putting in abstracts to talk about the project at conferences,..., I have my team reaching out to all sorts of entities...making them be aware of the project...trying to get the word out." (P9)
  1. Promoting an initiative online motivated people in other communities to initiate similar relief efforts
"I saw articles on how people were 3D printing face shields and other PPE masks and things for COVID...I was also sharing those articles with friends...so it was...what then exploded and turned into [our initiative]." (P1)
  1. Sharing open-source resources to make it easy for organizers in other local communities to launch a similar relief activity
"Very unique about this whole pandemic response is the open-source sort of response...around the world...there was this whole sort of global collaboration going on." (P1)

‍

Challenge: The pool of people who could provide support decreased due to the virus exposure

"They couldn’t help for their own safety. They wanted to, but they couldn’t, and it was hard on them." (P12)

→ Solution:

  1. Providing ways for volunteers to provide support without being in close contact with others
"It’s a way of community to come together and do something for people actually on the frontlines and be in the background...I did not have to physically do something. How easy can they make it for me to help in something like this....this was a no brainer for me." (P16)
  1. Adapting and improvising expertise to the unusual disaster situation
Local distilleries in P2’s community re-purposed their equipment to distill hand sanitizer;
a family business that originally made snow skis switched over and produced plastic face shields

‍

3. Volunteers Joining Relief Efforts

Volunteers (Joiners) joining relief efforts and leading other efforts (Initiators)

“[Anonymized], she’s the one who started that. She approached us. She is very community-oriented. She...[reached] out to some other restaurants in the area to be involved in a partnership...she spearheaded that and she did a phenomenal job...we did not go out and approach them. They came on board when [anonymized] got the ball rolling.” (P10)

Motivations 1: Connectedness and belonging to the local community

“they wanted to...give back to the communities...[because they] cared about people living in their communities” (P10)

Motivations 2: Attachment to local places

“A lot of the restaurants we work with are really our neighbors. So people wanted to keep their businesses flowing. It’s what people feel very attached to, knowing that the place was where you were getting your morning coffee for ten years, knowing that that place might not survive the crisis and being given an avenue to support them...I think that was really attractive to people.” (P13)

‍

4. Broader Impacts of Coproduced Disaster Relief

Civic initiatives were aimed at specific disaster relief outcomes (e.g., attenuating food insecurity, addressing PPE shortages). In addition to achieving their missions, they also had broader positive impacts on individual volunteers and the wider community.

Individual Level: Promoting Psychological Well-being of Individuals

Before Joining Initiatives: Many got laid off and social activities that reminded them that they mattered suddenly disappeared; felt depressive

felt “anxious and helpless” (P13)
“[I felt] so bad and [wanted] to do something” (P16)
felt bad when “frontline workers [were] working hard” (P14)

After Joining Initiatives: Volunteers felt rewarding and felt good for making contributions to the community

“it was nice to see that...I can do something to help” (P8)
‍
“Providing the funds [gave] a sense of helping the healthcare workers in a way that [we] would not have been able to do so before.” (P15)
‍
“Extra things we did...seem to be appreciated by the community...we feel good about what we’ve done...to help the community out” (P17)

After Joining Initiatives: Organizers and volunteers received appreciation from the people they helped, which started a cycle of prosocial behaviors and had a longer-lasting impact throughout the community.

A local grocery store purchased meals from P17's restaurant to thank their staff for doing essential work during the pandemic. P17 joined the act of appreciation by writing a thank-you card with an uplifting message for grocery store workers, and they visited her restaurant and appreciated her back: “Being part of a greater cause has come back ten fold...We showed our appreciation, and then they showed their appreciation to us...small acts of appreciation...can go a long way” (P17)

‍

Community Level: A Starting Point to Serving Local Community and Beyond

Before COVID-19, although people knew that vulnerable members had always existed, they did not take the issue seriously enough to take action. COVID-19 made people recognize and start providing support for vulnerable community members who had been easily unnoticed and been left out of society.

The original intention of P13's initiative was to feed healthcare workers and revive local businesses, but they expanded their attention “more and more to supporting people with severe food insecurity or house insecurity” (P13)
‍
“There always, unfortunately, will be people in need, there are always people who want to help. So we are aiming to continue this effort far beyond COVID-19.” (P3)

Civic initiatives also helped local communities realize and appreciate community service workers who were often under-recognized, such as those at testing centers, postal workers, and social workers.

P17 hoped that even after the pandemic, “just regular everyday, people remember about...anybody that is in a social service role [who] does thousands of work for the community”
‍
“They should continue with it even after COVID-19... lots of people do lots of things in our community that make it worth living...we can appreciate other groups of people doing so much to our community.” (P16)

Design Recommendations

I proposed a socio-technical platform that can mobilize and record tangible disaster relief activities with design features that emerged from the findings, which are to facilitate the initiation, development, and sustainability of coproductive disaster relief.

1. Designs for the Initiation of Coproductive Disaster Relief

☹️ Pain Points
🧠 Design Recommendation
Disasters restricts in-person interactions and many relief efforts are unnoticed to potential volunteers
• An open platform that compiles an integrated list of ongoing relief activities; Users can publish a relief activity project with a description of its aims and the required resources or skills, and other users can join
• Allow flexibility in redesigning existing activities and enable joiners to build a sub-project derived from an original one, to ensure that a joiner may also become an initiator
Hard to identify individuals with certain resources or expertise appropriate for a relief effort, while civic initiatives necessitate volunteers with different skills and resources coming together
• User profiles with skill and resource tags, to automatically match and recommend suitable projects or to offer relief groups a list of users with relevant capabilities to invite to their relief efforts
Individuals not realizing whether they were capable of supporting others or how to do so
• Show users how other users with similar skill and resource tags adapted their skills/resources to provide relief efforts
• Publish daily impact updates to make each effort more visible

2. Designs for the Development of Coproductive Disaster Relief

☹️ Pain Points
🧠 Design Recommendation
There is no one-size-fits-all disaster relief activity; they should be localized
• Branches for each relief effort can be added by joiners, describing adjustments made to localize the original relief activity, which can be referred to others to understand how the relief activity was applied in various settings
Immediate actions needed; no time to make a needed resource from scratch
• Commons-based peer production features, like GitHub, to help multiple contributors to incrementally enhance shared commons and expedite the coordination of relief efforts
• A platform like this can be served as a repository of disaster relief, so people can see how similar crises in the past were handled

3. Designs for the Sustainability of Coproductive Disaster Relief

☹️ Pain Points
🧠 Design Recommendation
People feeling hopeless when a disaster strikes
• The platform dedicated to coproductive disaster relief can make an effort that is often hidden more visible, increasing collective efficacy, the belief that the community can overcome crises successfully
Hard to sustain volunteers motivated, especially when it is a long-term disaster
• Push notifications to prompt users to reciprocate gratitude, which can increase recognition, reputation, and social ties

Future Research Insights

Coproduction of Civic Disaster Relief in Other Countries

This paper is a starting point for applying a lens of coproduction to interpret citizen-based disaster relief efforts and to see that coproductive disaster relief does happen. Non-U.S.-based local communities may have different types or levels of civic disaster relief. Future work could explore how other civic initiatives in other cultures coproduced relief efforts and their impacts to see if new themes arise.

Design Probes

• Realize design recommendations suggested in this study and collect feedback from disaster relief organizers and volunteers
